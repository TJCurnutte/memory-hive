# Synthesis Framework
*Research-Analyst Agent — Working Reference*

---

## 1. Intake — Parsing the Research Question

Before collecting a single source, answer:

- **What exactly does the client/questioner need to know?** (Not just the topic — the decision or action it informs)
- **What is the scope?** (Time-bound? Geographic? Industry? One specific angle or landscape?)
- **Who is the audience?** (Expert? Executive? Non-specialist? This changes everything.)
- **What is the deadline?** (Shapes depth and delivery format.)
- **What does "done" look like?** (A yes/no answer? A ranked list? A narrative briefing?)

> *If you can't explain the research question in one sentence, you don't understand it yet.*

---

## 2. Collection — Organizing Raw Information

### Note-Taking Standards
- Each note: source title, URL, date, credibility rating, 1-3 key findings, your own tags
- Use a consistent tagging taxonomy: `#market`, `#risk`, `#technology`, `#regulation`, `#timeline`, `#disputed`
- Tag each note with **confidence level**: High / Medium / Low (and why)
- Track open questions alongside facts — gaps are as important as findings

### Source Matrix Template
```
| Source | Type | Credibility | Key Finding | Tags | Confidence |
|--------|------|-------------|-------------|------|------------|
```

### What to Capture
- Direct quotes for striking language or precise definitions
- Statistics with sample sizes and methodology notes
- Named experts and their institutional affiliations
- Dates — when was this information valid? Has it aged?

---

## 3. Analysis — From Information to Insight

### Pattern Recognition
- Group notes by theme. What keeps appearing across independent sources?
- Identify **consensus points** (most sources agree) — lead with these
- Identify **debate points** (sources disagree) — surface fairly, don't editorialize
- Look for **structural forces** — what's driving the trend, not just what is happening
- Find **anomalies** — outliers that don't fit the pattern — these are often the most valuable insight

### Levels of Analysis
1. **Descriptive**: What happened / what is
2. **Diagnostic**: Why it happened / why it is
3. **Predictive**: What likely happens next
4. **Prescriptive**: What to do about it

> *Most briefings stop at level 1 or 2. If the audience needs action, push to level 3–4.*

### Identifying Gaps
- What did you search for but couldn't find?
- What perspectives are missing from your sources?
- What questions remain unanswered?
- Be explicit about gaps — they build credibility and guide future research.

## 4. Structuring — Briefing Formats

### Short Briefing (1 page / 5 min read)
```
[1 sentence conclusion — the bottom line up front]
Key Facts (3-5 bullets)
What Changed Recently (1-2 bullets)
What to Watch (2-3 bullets)
Sources (compact list)
```

### Medium Briefing (2-4 pages / 10-15 min read)
```
EXECUTIVE SUMMARY (5-7 sentences — fully self-contained)
  → Reader should get the key answer without reading further

SITUATION OVERVIEW
  → Context and current state

KEY FINDINGS
  → 3-5 named sections, each with a clear sub-header
  → One main insight per section

ANALYSIS & IMPLICATIONS
  → What this means for the reader's context

GAPS & OPEN QUESTIONS
  → What you couldn't confirm or what's still unresolved

SOURCES
  → Formatted citations, quality-rated
```

### Long Briefing / Report (10+ pages)
```
TITLE + METADATA (date, scope, author, audience, version)

EXECUTIVE SUMMARY (≤1 page)

TABLE OF CONTENTS

1. BACKGROUND / CONTEXT
2. METHODOLOGY NOTE (what you searched, how you vetted)
3. FINDINGS (numbered sections by theme)
4. ANALYSIS
5. STAKEHOLDER IMPLICATIONS (different audiences — what each needs to know)
6. GAPS & LIMITATIONS
7. RECOMMENDATIONS (if requested)
8. SOURCES & METHODOLOGY

APPENDICES (data tables, long quotes, methodology details)
```

---

## 5. Writing — Plain Language Principles

### Core Rules
- **Lead with the conclusion.** Don't make readers hunt for the answer.
- **One idea per paragraph.** Each paragraph does one job.
- **Active voice.** "The company announced..." not "It was announced by the company..."
- **Define jargon** on first use. Don't assume shared vocabulary.
- **Concrete over abstract.** "Revenue fell 12%" beats "Revenue performance declined materially."

### Hedging Correctly
| Situation | How to Hedge |
|---|---|
| Well-supported consensus | "The evidence strongly suggests..." |
| Emerging but not confirmed | "Early signals indicate..." |
| Single source, unverified | "According to [source], ..." |
| Speculation | "If this trend continues, ... (this is projection, not fact)" |
| Disputed / contested | "Sources disagree on...; the dominant view is..., while others argue..." |

### Avoiding Info Overload
- If a section has more than 7 bullet points, group into sub-themes
- If a briefing exceeds 4 pages without an executive summary, restructure
- Kill every sentence that doesn't support the core answer
- Use tables for comparisons, timelines for sequences, lists for enumeration

---

## 6. Delivery — Tailoring for Audiences

| Audience | Tone | Length | Emphasis |
|---|---|---|---|
| **C-suite / Exec** | Conclusions first, minimal jargon | 1-2 pages max | Business implications, risk, opportunity |
| **Technical / Specialist** | Precise, methodology-aware | Medium-long | Accuracy, nuance, caveats |
| **Client / Non-specialist** | Plain language, context-rich | Medium | Why this matters to them specifically |
| **Internal team / Peer** | Efficient, assume baseline knowledge | Short-medium | Actionable, debate-ready |

### Visual Aids
- **Timeline charts** for sequences, roadmaps, historical context
- **Tables** for comparisons, rankings, data summaries
- **Infographics** sparingly — only when the visual adds comprehension beyond text
- Always include source attribution on any data visualization

### Citation Standards
- Inline: `[Author, Year]` or `[Source Name, Date]`
- Full citations in Sources section: author, title, publication, date, URL
- Rate source quality: `★★★` peer-reviewed, `★★` established org, `★` single source / unverified

---

## 7. Quality Checklist — Before Delivery

- [ ] Does the executive summary stand alone? (Can someone get the answer without reading further?)
- [ ] Is every factual claim backed by a source?
- [ ] Are all sources credible-rated?
- [ ] Have you surfaced disagreements, not just consensus?
- [ ] Have you noted gaps and unresolved questions?
- [ ] Is the audience and use case clear in the framing?
- [ ] Have you removed jargon that wasn't defined?
- [ ] Have you checked: is the hedging accurate to the evidence strength?
- [ ] Does the structure match the briefing format (short/medium/long)?
- [ ] Have you done a "steel man" pass — argued against your own conclusions?
- [ ] Is the document free of your own opinion unless explicitly labeled as such?
- [ ] Are all links and citations live / accurate?
