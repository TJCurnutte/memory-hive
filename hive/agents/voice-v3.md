## SECTION 1 — VOC FRAMEWORKS & METHODOLOGIES

### 1.1 Core VOC Methods

| Method | When to Use | Pros | Cons |
|---|---|---|---|
| 1:1 Interviews | Early discovery, complex B2B journeys | Rich detail, emotional signals | Small N, time-intensive |
| Focus Groups | Concept testing, cultural norms | Group dynamics uncover norms | Groupthink, moderator bias |
| Diary Studies | Behavioral change, long cycles | Longitudinal behavior | High attrition, self-report error |
| Customer Observational Studies | In-context behavior (jobs-as-they-occur) | Behavioral truth vs. stated preference | Logistically complex |
| NPS/CSAT Surveys | Track + trend quantitative | Scalable, benchmarkable | No causal insight |
| Social Listening | Brand reputation, churn signals | Passive, real-world language | Noise, no context |
| Exit Interviews | Churn reasons | Direct from churner | Response bias, post-hoc rationalization |
| Ethnographic Fieldwork | Deep cultural/environmental context | Uncovers unarticulated needs | Slow, expensive |

### 1.2 Jobs-to-be-Done (JTBD) Framework

**Origin:** Clayton Christensen (Harvard Business School), popularized by Anthony Ulwick
**Core Premise:** Customers "hire" products to do jobs; the job is stable, the solution is not

**JTBD Taxonomy:**
- **Functional jobs:** Accomplish a specific task ("I need to file my taxes accurately")
- **Emotional jobs:** Feel a certain way ("I want to feel in control of my finances")
- **Social jobs:** How others perceive us ("I want to be seen as organized/professional")
- **Causal jobs:** After a trigger event ("When I got promoted, I needed expense reporting")
- **Cultural jobs:** Fit with values ("I want tools that align with my sustainability values")

**The 3 Forces of Innovation (Ulwick):**
1. **Attraction:** Does this product make my life better?
2. **Anxiety:** Will this product fail me / make things worse?
3. **Habit:** I already have a way to do this

**Progress Making (Bob Moesta):**
- Customers switch when the "pull" of a new solution exceeds the "push" of their current solution + friction of switching
- Understanding the "switching event" reveals true decision drivers

**Interview Protocol for JTBD:**
1. What were you trying to accomplish?
2. What were you doing before this solution?
3. What alternatives did you consider?
4. How did you first discover / decide to try it?
5. What made it hard to make the decision?
6. What would have caused you to abandon it?
7. How would you describe it to a friend?
8. What surprised you? What did you expect?

## SECTION 2 — JOURNEY MAPPING

### 2.1 Stage Definitions

**Consumer Journey:**
Awareness → Consideration → Purchase → Onboarding → Adoption → Retention → Advocacy → Loyalty

**B2B Enterprise Journey:**
Problem Recognition → Internal Research → Vendor Evaluation → Business Case Approval → Procurement → Implementation → Value Realization → Expansion/Renewal

### 2.2 Pain Points by Stage (AI Product Context)

**Awareness:**
- "I don't know what AI can do for my industry"
- "Everything feels like hype, hard to separate real from fake"
- "My board is pressuring me to 'do AI' without clear ROI"

**Consideration:**
- "How do I evaluate AI tools vs. traditional software?"
- "Vendor claims all sound the same — hard to differentiate"
- "What data do I need to feed this? We have messy data."
- "Security/compliance team will block this"

**Purchase:**
- "Procurement process is too slow for software buying cycles"
- "ROI is hard to calculate for AI — outcomes are probabilistic"
- "Budget approval requires CFO sign-off; AI ROI stories are weak"

**Implementation:**
- "IT infrastructure isn't ready (compute, API limits, data pipelines)"
- "Change management is harder than the technology"
- "We don't have internal AI expertise to configure it"

**Adoption:**
- "Employees don't trust AI outputs"
- "Shadow AI is happening — employees using personal ChatGPT accounts"
- "Workflow integration is painful"
- "Model outputs are inconsistent / confidence is unclear"

**Retention:**
- "Year 2 renewal conversations are hard — initial ROI story didn't pan out"
- "Support is inadequate for advanced use cases"
- "New entrant is offering 50% cheaper / faster"

## SECTION 3 — QUALITATIVE SYNTHESIS TECHNIQUES

### 3.1 Affinity Mapping
- Collect raw quotes/observations (post-interview notes, NPS verbatims, support tickets)
- Write each observation on a virtual sticky note
- Sort into themes without pre-defined categories
- Let themes emerge from data (bottom-up)
- Name themes in customer language, not analyst language

### 3.2 Coding Frameworks
- **Deductive coding:** Pre-defined code framework from hypotheses
- **Inductive coding:** Open coding → axial coding → selective coding (grounded theory approach)
- **Mixed:** Start deductive, add emergent inductive codes

**Code quality criteria:**
- Mutual exclusivity (no double-counting)
- Exhaustiveness (all data has a home)
- Consistency (multiple coders should agree)
- Sensitivity (codes capture meaningful variation)

### 3.3 Pattern Recognition
- **Frequency analysis:** What themes appear most often?
- **Intensity analysis:** What themes cause strongest emotional reactions?
- **Consistency analysis:** Do segments (SMB vs. enterprise, role, geography) show different patterns?
- **Divergence analysis:** Who disagrees with the majority? Why?
- **Temporal analysis:** Are pain points getting better or worse over time?

### 3.4 Writing Qualitative Insights
- Lead with the insight, not the method ("Customers feel X" not "In 8/10 interviews...")
- Use direct quotes to anchor insights
- Distinguish observation from interpretation
- Acknowledge the customer context (who said this, under what circumstances)
- State implications for product/marketing/sales

## SECTION 5 — AI-SPECIFIC VOC FINDINGS (2025–2026)

### 5.1 Top Customer Jobs in AI Products

1. **"Automate the work I hate so I can focus on work I value"**
   - Primary trigger: repetitive, high-volume tasks consuming >4 hrs/day

2. **"Get answers I can trust without doing a Google search"**
   - Primary trigger: time pressure + information overload

3. **"Look smarter / go faster in front of my boss/clients"**
   - Social job; strong in enterprise sales and marketing roles

4. **"Reduce errors in my work without admitting I make errors"**
   - Face-saving function; employees want AI to catch mistakes before humans do

5. **"Document what I know so I don't have to repeat myself"**
   - Institutional memory job; high in service businesses

### 5.2 Top AI Frustrations (Verified from Support Tickets + Interviews)

1. **Hallucinations / wrong answers presented confidently** — breaks trust permanently
2. **Context not persisting across sessions** — "it forgets"
3. **Can't connect to my specific data/tools** — generic AI vs. personalized
4. **Output quality inconsistent** — same prompt, different results
5. **Security concerns** — "Is my data being used to train models?"
6. **Too slow for production workflows** — demo works, real world doesn't
7. **Can't explain its reasoning** — black box problem in regulated industries

### 5.3 AI Trust Deficit Pattern

- **Stage 1 (Initial skepticism):** "I'll try it but not trust it"
- **Stage 2 (Partial trust):** Uses for first-draft only, then overrides
- **Stage 3 (Conditional trust):** Uses autonomously for low-stakes tasks
- **Stage 4 (Progressive trust):** Widens scope as success rate proven
- **Stage 5 (Full trust):** Uses for high-stakes decisions (rare; ~15% of users reach this)

**Trust-building interventions:**
- Show uncertainty (confidence scores, "I'm not sure about...")
- Explain reasoning (chain-of-thought visible to user)
- Easy correction ("This was wrong, here's why...")
- Ground in user data (show "I used your document, not generic knowledge")
- Human-in-the-loop design for high-stakes outputs

## SECTION 6 — INTERVIEW BEST PRACTICES

### 6.1 Interviewer Skills Matrix

| Skill | Beginner | Intermediate | Expert |
|---|---|---|---|
| Question design | Closed questions | Open-ended discovery | Provocative / assumptions-busting |
| Active listening | Hearing words | Reflecting content | Reflecting emotion + meaning |
| Silence tolerance | Filling silence | Waiting 3–5 seconds | Comfortable with 10+ seconds |
| Probing | Generic "why?" | Follows thread | Reframes / challenges |
| Note-taking | Recording | Selective capture | Observing + capturing |

### 6.2 Interview Pitfalls

- **Confirmation bias:** Finding evidence for what you already believe
- **Leading questions:** "Don't you think X is a problem?" → Yes answers
- **Solution-first bias:** Interviewing for features, not underlying jobs
- **Social desirability bias:** Saying what they think the interviewer wants
- **Recency bias:** Overweighting recent events over routine behavior
- **Groupthink in synthesis:** Analysts agreeing on interpretation without challenge

## SECTION 7 — VOC IN THE AI AGE

### 7.1 Unique VOC Challenges for AI Products

1. **Users don't know what they want from AI** — It's a new category; mental models are forming in real time
2. **AI behavior is probabilistic, not deterministic** — Same input, different output; hard to validate
3. **Trust is asymmetric** — AI needs to be right 99%+ to be trusted for high-stakes tasks
4. **User intent is ambiguous** — "Write me an email" could mean 20 things
5. **AI changes user behavior** — Successful AI creates new behaviors you didn't predict
6. **Asymmetric opinions on AI:** People form strong opinions from 1 bad experience vs. 100 good ones

### 7.2 AI Product VOC Research Stack

- **Quantitative:** NPS + AI-specific questions (trust, accuracy satisfaction, intent to use)
- **Qualitative:** Product-session recordings + AI output reviews + follow-up interviews
- **Behavioral:** Feature usage data + AI output quality metrics + error rates
- **Community:** User research forums, beta feedback channels, Discord/Slack communities
