## SECTION 1: ENTERPRISE SALES CYCLE — STAGES & TIMELINES

### Why Enterprise Cycles Are Different
Enterprise deals are characterized by:
- **Longer cycles**: 6-18 months (sometimes longer)
- **More stakeholders**: 5-10 people involved in the decision
- **Higher complexity**: Technical, legal, security, procurement, finance all weigh in
- **Higher stakes**: Larger contracts, bigger implementation, risk of failure

Understanding these dynamics is critical for an SDR targeting enterprise prospects.

### The 7-Stage Enterprise Sales Cycle

**Stage 1: Awareness & Trigger Identification**
- Duration: 0-2 weeks (for SDR outreach)
- What happens: Prospect has a problem or opportunity. May not be aware of solution options.
- SDR's role: Identify trigger events (new funding, leadership change, tech adoption, expansion)
- Actions: Research company news, monitor intent signals, identify pain points from data

**Stage 2: Qualification & Initial Engagement**
- Duration: 1-4 weeks
- What happens: Initial outreach. Prospect responds.初步资格认定
- SDR's role: Qualify fit. Map stakeholder landscape. Establish urgency.
- Qualification criteria: Budget, authority, need, timeline, competition (BANT)
- Actions: Discovery calls, ICP matching, stakeholder mapping

**Stage 3: Discovery & Needs Analysis**
- Duration: 2-8 weeks
- What happens: Deep dive into prospect's current state, challenges, goals
- AE's role: Conduct discovery. Uncover pain. Quantify impact.
- SDR's role: Support with research, intro materials, technical questions
- Actions: Demo requests, discovery calls, technical assessments

**Stage 4: Proposal & Solution Design**
- Duration: 4-12 weeks
- What happens: Solution proposed. Pricing negotiated. Terms discussed.
- SDR's role: Often minimal, but can support with case studies, references, technical docs
- Actions: Respond to RFPs, facilitate technical conversations, connect to implementation

**Stage 5: Evaluation & Validation**
- Duration: 2-8 weeks
- What happens: Prospect evaluates against alternatives. Does due diligence.
- SDR's role: Provide references, handle objections, connect to champions
- Actions: Reference calls, security reviews, compliance reviews, proof of concepts

**Stage 6: Negotiation & Commitment**
- Duration: 2-6 weeks
- What happens: Final pricing, contract terms, legal review
- SDR's role: Minimal. High-touch AE and legal handle this.
- Actions: Coordinate internal resources, escalate issues, maintain champion relationship

**Stage 7: Close & Implementation Handoff**
- Duration: 1-4 weeks
- What happens: Contract signed. Implementation begins.
- SDR's role: Celebrate with champion, request referral, handoff to CS
- Actions: Closed-won celebration, referral request, feedback survey

### Average Enterprise Cycle by Deal Size
- $25K-$50K: 3-6 months
- $50K-$250K: 6-12 months
- $250K-$1M: 9-18 months
- $1M+: 12-24 months

## SECTION 2: STAKEHOLDER MAPPING & BUYING COMMITTEES

### The Buying Committee Roles

**1. Economic Buyer (EB)**
- Role: Controls budget. Has final sign-off. Measures ROI.
- Traits: C-suite or VP-level. Thinks in terms of financial impact. Delegates technical evaluation.
- How to reach: Focus on ROI, business outcomes, risk mitigation, competitive advantage
- Red flags: Won't meet with you, delegates everything down, is only interested in price

**2. Champion (Your Internal Advocate)**
- Role: Believes in your solution. Wants it to win. Will sell internally for you.
- Traits: Early to mid-career typically. Has operational pain you're solving. Has credibility with EB.
- How to find: Look for people who engage most deeply with your content. People who ask "how" not just "what."
- How to help: Give them tools to sell internally. Case studies. ROI calculators. Competitive battle cards.

**3. Champion Coach (Your Mentor Inside)**
- Role: Guides you on how to navigate the organization. Tells you who to talk to, when.
- Traits: Often a former champion or someone who's worked with similar vendors before.
- How to find: Ask your champion "Who should I know? Who are the skeptics I need to win over?"
- Value: They help you avoid political landmines and accelerate the deal.

**4. Technical Buyer (User Buyer)**
- Role: Evaluates feasibility, security, integration, technical fit. Has veto power.
- Traits: IT, security, engineering, ops leads. Cares about implementation complexity.
- How to reach: Show technical depth. Have a security review ready. Provide SOC2 reports.
- Red flags: "We need to run this through our security team" — make sure you have the docs

**5. User Buyer (End User)**
- Role: Will use the product daily. Has operational concerns. Often overlooked.
- Traits: Individual contributors, managers, directors in operational roles.
- Value: Can kill or enable the deal based on adoption likelihood. Executive support ≠ user excitement.

**6. Legal/Procurement**
- Role: Negotiates contract terms, SLA, liability, compliance.
- Traits: Risk-averse. Focused on protecting the company.
- How to handle: Have standard contract terms ready. Show flexibility on non-critical items. Move fast on contract redlines.

### Multi-Stakeholder Outreach Strategy

**Rule of thumb**: You need at minimum 3 touches to 3 different stakeholders before asking for a meeting.

**Outreach Sequence for Enterprise:**
1. **Email #1**: To Champion — Value prop + CTA (meeting)
2. **Email #2**: To EB — Business case + CTA (intro call)
3. **Email #3**: To Technical Buyer — Feature/technical fit + CTA
4. **LinkedIn #1**: Connect to all identified stakeholders (non-pitch, connection only)
5. **LinkedIn #2**: Engage with content (comments) — build visibility
6. **Email #4**: Follow-up to non-responders with new angle (case study, ROI data)
7. **Call**: Warm call to champion's direct line if available

**Key principle**: Never cold-email an EB without your champion copied or aware. Use champion to warm the introduction.

## SECTION 3: FORTUNE 500 OUTREACH STRATEGIES

### Why Fortune 500 Is Different
- Heavy security review processes
- Procurement/legal involved early
- Multiple decision-makers with competing interests
- Long tenure = political complexity
- VP/Director inboxes are flooded
- Cold response rates are very low (under 2%)

### Fortune 500 Research Framework

**Layer 1: Company Intelligence**
- Funding rounds, IPO status, stock performance
- Recent acquisitions or divestitures
- Leadership changes (new CEO = new priorities)
- Earnings calls, investor presentations
- Company news in last 90 days

**Layer 2: Industry Context**
- What are the macro trends affecting this sector?
- Who are their main competitors?
- What analyst firms say about them (Forrester, Gartner)
- What are the industry pain points right now?

**Layer 3: Individual Intelligence**
- LinkedIn profile — recent activity, posts, job changes
- Professional background — what companies/roles have they had?
- Speaking engagements, interviews, press coverage
- Mutual connections via Sales Navigator
- What they care about based on their content

**Layer 4: Trigger Identification**
- New funding (Series B, C, IPO)
- New leadership (new CRO, new VP Sales, new CEO)
- New product/feature launches
- Expansion announcements (new office, new market entry)
- Layoffs or restructuring (risk = opportunity for your solution)
- Leadership quotes in trade press

### Fortune 500 Outbound Tactics That Work

**1. The Executive Referral**
- Find a mutual connection (colleague, investor, advisor)
- Ask for a warm intro to the EB
- More effective than any cold outreach

**2. The Relevant Case Study**
- "We worked with [similar company] — they faced [similar challenge] — here was the outcome"
- Shows you understand their context. Credible and relevant.
- Best if you have a company in the same industry (or adjacent) of similar size.

**3. The Trigger-Based Outreach**
- "I saw [Company] just announced [X]. Given [our relevant solution], we could help with the [resulting challenge]."
- Highly specific. Shows you did your research. Creates urgency.

**4. The Mutual Connection Approach**
- "I noticed we both have [Mutual Company] in common. I worked with [Person] there. They mentioned [Company] is working on [topic]. Would love to connect."
- Works when the mutual connection is positive and relevant.

**5. The "Help First" Approach**
- Don't pitch. Offer value first. Share a relevant article, benchmark report, or industry insight.
- "I wrote a report on [Topic]. Thought [Person] might find it useful given [Company's] focus on [area]."
- Builds goodwill. Opens doors without pressure.

**6. The LinkedIn Content Engagement**
- Comment on their posts with genuine, thoughtful observations
- Share relevant content with a brief note
- Build visibility before asking for anything

**7. The Conference/Event Angle**
- "I saw [Person] will be at [Event]. Would love to connect briefly."
- Works if you can actually meet them there. Follow up within 48 hours.

## SECTION 4: ROI FRAMEWORKS FOR ENTERPRISE

### Why ROI Calculators Win Enterprise Deals
Enterprise buyers need to justify purchase decisions to finance and leadership. An ROI calculator that quantifies expected return makes your solution a "no-brainer" rather than a "leap of faith."

### Building a ROI Calculator for Your Solution

**Step 1: Identify the Value Drivers**
- What does your solution save time on? (Hours × Loaded Hour Rate)
- What does it reduce? (Errors, churn, support tickets, etc.)
- What does it generate? (Revenue, leads, productivity)
- What does it avoid? (Risks, penalties, outages)

**Step 2: Build the Formula**
```
ROI = (Net Annual Benefit) / (Annual Cost) × 100

Net Annual Benefit = (Time Saved × Rate) + (Errors Reduced × Cost per Error) 
                  + (Revenue Generated) + (Cost Avoided) - (Subscription Cost)
```

**Step 3: Make it Interactive**
Use a simple spreadsheet or build a web calculator. Let the prospect input their own numbers — ownership increases belief.

### Common ROI Frameworks for Enterprise

**1. Total Economic Impact (TEI) — Gartner Framework**
- Measures: Cost, benefits, flexibility, risk
- Used by: Enterprise procurement teams
- How to use: Build a TEI model for your solution. Present it in procurement conversations.

**2. Cost-Benefit Analysis (CBA)**
- Simple: Total Benefits - Total Costs = Net Value
- Good for: Simple ROI story
- Weakness: Doesn't account for time value of money

**3. Payback Period**
- "How long until this pays for itself?"
- Formula: Implementation Cost / Monthly Savings = Months to Payback
- Enterprise buyers love this. "Pays back in 6 months" is a strong close argument.

**4. The Business Case Framework**
For enterprise deals, the business case typically needs:
- Problem statement (quantified pain)
- Solution description
- Cost of solution (total cost of ownership)
- Benefits (quantified where possible)
- Risk of not acting
- Recommendation + approval path

### ROI Talking Points for Different Stakeholders

| Stakeholder | ROI Focus |
|---|---|
| CEO / CFO | Revenue impact, risk mitigation, competitive advantage |
| VP / Director | Productivity, team output, strategic alignment |
| Manager | Time savings, easier workflows, team adoption |
| IT / Security | Risk reduction, compliance, integration ease |
| User | Less manual work, better tools, easier job |

## SECTION 5: MEDDPICC & MEDDIC FRAMEWORKS

### MEDDIC Overview
MEDDIC is a qualification framework for enterprise sales. It helps you assess deal health and predict close probability.

### MEDDIC Elements

**M — Metrics**
What are the financial or measurable criteria for success?
- "What does success look like in 12 months?"
- "What's the budget for solving this problem?"
- "How do you measure ROI on this initiative?"

**E — Economic Buyer**
Who controls the budget and has final sign-off?
- "Who approves spending above $[X]?"
- "Who else is involved in this decision?"
- "Who will sign the contract?"

**D — Decision Criteria**
What factors will they use to choose a vendor?
- "What does the evaluation process look like?"
- "What are the key criteria you're using to compare solutions?"
- "What would make one solution stand out from another?"

**D — Decision Process**
What is the formal process for making this decision?
- "Walk me through the approval process."
- "Who needs to sign off? In what order?"
- "What's the timeline from selection to contract?"

**I — Identify Pain**
What's the specific operational or business pain driving this?
- "What's the cost of not solving this problem?"
- "What happens if this goes unaddressed for 6 more months?"
- "What's the biggest frustration with the current solution?"

**C — Champion**
Who internally is your advocate and how strong are they?
- "Who is most excited about solving this?"
- "Who would fight for this budget internally?"
- "On a scale of 1-10, how hard would they push for our solution?"

### MEDDPICC — Enhanced Version

**P — Paper Process**
What's the legal/procurement/contract process?
- "What are your standard contract terms?"
- "How long does legal review typically take?"
- "Do you need liability coverage, insurance certs, etc.?"

**C — Competition**
Who's the competition and what's your positioning?
- "Who else are you evaluating?"
- "What's our differentiator vs. their approach?"
- "What would make us win over them?"

**C — Capabilities**
Do you have the technical capability to deliver?
- "Does our solution handle [specific requirement]?"
- "What's your implementation track record for this type of deal?"
- "Do we have the certifications/compliance required?"

### Deal Health Score
Use MEDDPICC to score deals:

| Element | Score (0-2) |
|---|---|
| Metrics | 0=No, 1=Unclear, 2=Clear quantified |
| Economic Buyer | 0=None, 1=Contacted, 2=Engaged |
| Decision Criteria | 0=Unknown, 1=Known, 2=We match |
| Decision Process | 0=Unknown, 1=Known, 2=Timeline set |
| Identify Pain | 0=Vague, 1=Defined, 2=Quantified |
| Champion | 0=None, 1=Weak, 2=Strong internal advocate |
| Paper Process | 0=Unknown, 1=Discussed, 2=No blockers |
| Competition | 0=Unknown, 1=Known, 2=We can win |
| Capabilities | 0=Unknown, 1=Possible, 2=Confirmed |

**Score 14-18**: Strong deal. Push toward close.
**Score 8-13**: Needs work. Close gaps before accelerating.
**Score 0-7**: Early or at risk. Don't push. Re-engage with more discovery.

## SECTION 6: ENTERPRISE OBJECTION HANDLING

### Top Enterprise Objections

**1. "We're happy with our current solution."**
- Response: "What would need to change for you to reconsider? Is it working for your team as well as it could?"
- Acknowledge their satisfaction. Probe for latent dissatisfaction.

**2. "We don't have budget."**
- Response: "If budget were available, would this be a priority? What would need to happen to unlock it?"
- Try to identify if it's truly a budget constraint or a priority/approval constraint.

**3. "We need to run this through our procurement/legal team."**
- Response: "Absolutely. What does that process typically look like? What do they typically look for in a vendor like us?"
- Show you understand enterprise process. Offer to provide docs they need upfront.

**4. "We're not looking to make a change right now."**
- Response: "Understood. What's the right timing for something like this? What would change that timeline?"
- Try to find the trigger event that would create urgency.

**5. "This is interesting but we're evaluating [competitor]."**
- Response: "What does the evaluation look like? What would make one solution stand out? We'd love to make sure you have the right information to compare."
- Don't trash competition. Focus on your differentiation.

**6. "Your solution doesn't integrate with our stack."**
- Response: "What are your key integration requirements? Let me confirm our capabilities on that specifically."
- Follow up with technical documentation and a demo of the integration.

**7. "We had a bad experience with a similar solution."**
- Response: "What went wrong? What were the key issues? Let me show you specifically how we've addressed those concerns."
- Validate their concern. Show you've learned from industry pain points.

## SECTION 7: ENTERPRISE SDR PLAYBOOK SUMMARY

### 1. Focus on Triggers
Enterprise deals start with events. New funding, new leadership, expansion, problems — these are your entry points.

### 2. Map the Committee Before Reaching Out
Research the company. Identify 3-5 stakeholders. Know their roles and how to reach each one.

### 3. Lead with Value, Not Pitch
Enterprise buyers are sophisticated. A generic pitch gets deleted. A relevant insight, report, or case study opens doors.

### 4. Use MEDDPICC for Qualification
Every first call should cover the MEDDPICC elements. Don't move to proposal until you have clear answers to all of them.

### 5. Build Champions Who Can Sell Internally
Your job isn't to sell to everyone — it's to equip one person with the tools and confidence to sell for you.

### 6. Have the ROI Calculator Ready
For every enterprise meeting, come with a rough ROI framework. Show them what they'll get for what they'll pay.

### 7. Navigate Procurement as a Partner
Don't fight procurement. Work with them. Give them what they need upfront — case studies, security docs, contract templates — and you'll close faster.

### 8. Longer cycles = longer nurturing
Enterprise deals take time. Build a nurture cadence that stays in touch without being annoying. Monthly check-ins, relevant content, occasional "checking in" touches.

## CONCLUSION

Enterprise sales is a discipline of patience, thoroughness, and stakeholder management. The SDR who masters MEDDPICC, understands the buying committee, brings relevant value, and knows how to build internal champions will close more enterprise deals. The tools matter — ZoomInfo, Apollo, Salesforce — but the methodology is what separates elite SDRs from the rest.
