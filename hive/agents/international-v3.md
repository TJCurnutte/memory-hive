## 1. International B2B Sales Fundamentals

### Why Go International?
- TAM expansion: Access markets 5-10× your home market.
- Customer diversification: Reduce dependence on one economy.
- Revenue diversification: Balance against domestic slowdowns.
- Talent sourcing: Access to global hiring pools.
- Competitive moat: If competitors are domestic-only, global reach = advantage.

### Market Prioritization Framework
Evaluate each market on:
1. **Market size**: Total addressable market, growth rate.
2. **Ease of entry**: Language barriers, regulatory complexity, time zones.
3. **Competitive intensity**: How many players? Differentiated opportunity?
4. **Revenue potential**: Deal size, sales cycle length, win rates.
5. **Infrastructure**: Digital adoption, internet penetration, tech sophistication.

## 2. Multi-Language Outreach

### Language Strategy
- **English-first markets**: UK, Netherlands, Nordics, India, Philippines, Nigeria. Can run English sequences.
- **Local language required**: Germany (German), France (French), Brazil (Portuguese), Spain (Spanish), Japan (Japanese), China (Mandarin + WeChat).
- **Hybrid approach**: English outreach with local language on follow-up or LinkedIn.

### Translation vs. Transcreation
- **Translation**: Word-for-word conversion. Fine for simple messages. Poor for sales copy.
- **Transcreation**: Meaning preserved, culturally adapted. Required for effective sales outreach.
- Always use native speakers or professional transcreation services for sales copy.

### Multi-Language Sequence Tips
- Keep email sequences shorter for non-English speakers (150-200 words max).
- Use local date formats in follow-up emails.
- Reference local events, regulations, news.
- Include local case studies or logos from the same region.
- Sign emails in local language for warm touch (even just "Saludos" / "Cordialement").

## 3. Cultural Differences in Sales

### High-Context vs. Low-Context Cultures (Geert Hofstede Framework)

**Low-Context Cultures** (Direct, Explicit)
- USA, Germany, Netherlands, Nordics, Switzerland
- Communication: Direct, to the point, efficient.
- Expectation: Get to the point, value facts.
- Risk: Being too informal can hurt credibility.

**High-Context Cultures** (Indirect, Relationship-First)
- Japan, China, Saudi Arabia, Brazil, Mexico, France
- Communication: Relationship before business, indirect.
- Expectation: Build trust first, read between the lines.
- Risk: Being too transactional damages the relationship.

### Regional Sales Behaviors

#### United States
- Fast-paced, efficiency-focused.
- "What's the ROI?" — expect direct financial questions.
- Informal: First-name basis quickly.
- Decision often made by consensus but speed is high.

#### Germany / DACH
- Formal until invited otherwise. Use "Herr/Frau" + last name initially.
- Data-driven: Bring numbers, benchmarks, case studies.
- Long sales cycles: 3-12 months common.
- GDPR is serious: Be careful with data collection.
- Quality over price: "Made in Germany" mindset applies to buying too.

#### United Kingdom
- Closest to US in approach, but more understated.
- "Not bad" means "quite good" — read subtext.
- Hierarchy matters more than in US.
- Punctuality is important.

#### France
- Relationship-first: Invest in rapport building.
- Formal: Use professional titles until invited to be informal.
- French language strongly preferred in B2B.
- Long lunches are part of the culture — work through them.
- Intellectual debate appreciated; don't oversimplify.

#### Nordics (Sweden, Norway, Denmark)
- Flat hierarchies — don't over-market or be too pushy.
- Very direct and efficient; minimal small talk.
- Decisions by consensus can be slow.
- Work-life balance is valued — don't contact outside business hours.

#### Brazil
- Warm and relationship-driven. Expect personal connection.
- Portuguese required for true penetration; English works at enterprise.
- "Jeitinho brasileiro" — finding creative workarounds is valued.
- Business happens in person when possible.
- Time is flexible — meetings may run over.

#### Mexico / LATAM
- Relationship-based: Invest in getting to know them.
- Spanish required for SDR-level; English works at VP+.
- "Hora mexicana" (Mexican time) — meetings may start 15-30 min late.
- Hierarchy matters; CC the decision-maker.
- Family/personal context is appropriate to ask about briefly.

#### Japan
- Extremely formal. First contact via email (formal), not cold calling.
- Business cards (meishi) exchange is a ritual.
- "Hai" (yes) does not always mean agreement — read context.
- Long decision cycles: 6-18 months common.
- Small yes, group consensus: Everyone has veto power.
- Gifts may be appropriate; avoid sets of 4 (death symbol).

#### India
- Hierarchy-driven: Address the most senior person.
- Relationship-based but increasingly transactional at enterprise.
- English widely spoken in business.
- Price-sensitive market; ROI must be crystal clear.
- Many decision-makers; identify all stakeholders.

#### Australia
- Similar to UK/US but more laid-back.
- Direct but not aggressive. "No worries" is common.
- Expect wit and humor in communication.
- Time zone advantage for US companies.

## 4. EMEA Outreach Strategies

### EMEA Sales Regions
- **UK**: Largest market, English-speaking, US-style sales motion.
- **DACH (Germany, Austria, Switzerland)**: German language required, formal, data-driven.
- **France**: French language, relationship-first, formal.
- **Benelux (Netherlands, Belgium, Luxembourg)**: English-friendly, EU hub.
- **Nordics (Sweden, Norway, Denmark, Finland)**: English-friendly, efficient.
- **Southern Europe (Spain, Italy, Portugal)**: Spanish/Italian language, relationship-first.
- **CEE (Poland, Czech, Hungary, Romania)**: English + local, growth markets.

### EMEA Outreach Best Practices
- **Local business hours**: Always send in local time zones.
- **GDPR compliance**: EU data protection regulation — get consent, document it.
- **Local phone numbers**: Use local DIDs if possible. Don't call German numbers from a US cell.
- **LinkedIn localization**: Set your profile to target the country. Post in English + local language.
- **EU office**: Consider a European entity for tax/legal/contracting purposes.

### GDPR for SDRs
- **Lawful basis**: Legitimate interest or consent for cold outreach.
- **Opt-out**: Must honor unsubscribe requests immediately.
- **Data minimization**: Only collect what's needed.
- **Privacy notice**: Tell them who you are and why you have their data.
- ** DPA agreements**: If using a vendor (Apollo, ZoomInfo), ensure they have DPAs.

## 5. LATAM Sales Strategies

### Key LATAM Markets
- **Brazil**: Largest market, 200M+ population, Portuguese language.
- **Mexico**: Second largest, US nearshoring trend, Spanish language.
- **Colombia**: Growing tech ecosystem, Spanish language.
- **Argentina**: Tech talent hub, language + inflation complexity.
- **Chile**: Most developed market, strong fintech/retail sector.

### LATAM Sales Approach
- **Language**: Spanish or Portuguese required for true market penetration.
- **Time zones**: 
  - Brazil (BRT): 1-2 hours ahead of US EST.
  - Mexico: Same as US Central.
  - Colombia: Same as US EST.
  - Argentina: 2 hours ahead of US EST.
- **Relationship-first**: Invest in rapport. Get to know them as people.
- **In-person preference**: Face-to-face meetings carry more weight.
- **Payment complexity**: Local payment methods (PIX in Brazil, OXXO in Mexico).
- **Legal entity**: Often need a local entity or partner to close contracts.

### LATAM Outreach Tactics
- WhatsApp outreach is standard — get a local number.
- LinkedIn is growing but email still primary.
- Spanish transcreation over direct translation.
- Reference local brands, regulatory changes, market news.
- Be patient — sales cycles may be longer due to hierarchy + budget cycles.

## 6. Working with Distributors / Channel Partners

### When to Use Distributors
- Market requires local entity (legal/regulatory).
- You lack local sales team.
- Market is remote/time-zone challenging.
- They already have the relationships in your ICP.

### Distributor Evaluation Checklist
- [ ] Do they sell to your ICP? (vertical fit)
- [ ] What is their coverage in your target geography?
- [ ] Do they represent competitive or complementary products?
- [ ] What training/ enablement can you provide?
- [ ] What are their margin requirements?
- [ ] Do they have technical capability or just sales?
- [ ] What are their references in your space?
- [ ] What marketing/sales resources can they deploy?

### Channel Partner Program Structure
1. **Register/deal registration**: Protect deals from channel conflict.
2. **Margin/commission**: 15-30% margin common for VARs; 8-15% for referral.
3. **Lead distribution**: How do leads flow to partners?
4. **Enablement**: Training, certifications, co-marketing funds.
5. **Performance metrics**: Minimum commitments, renewal targets.

### Managing Distributor Relationships
- Quarterly business reviews.
- Shared pipeline visibility.
- Joint account planning.
- Marketing development funds (MDF).
- Clear rules of engagement: Who owns which deal stage?

## 8. EMEA Deep Dive: Country-by-Country

### Germany (DACH)
- **Language**: German required for true penetration. English acceptable at enterprise with German-speaking AE.
- **Culture**: Formal, methodical, data-driven. "German engineering" applies to buying too — they want details.
- **Regulation**: GDPR is enforced aggressively. Get DPAs with all vendors. Consent-based outreach.
- **Legal entity**: GmbH structure commonly needed. VAT ID required for invoicing.
- **Sales cycle**: 3-12 months typical. Plan accordingly.
- **Best practices**: 
  - Send in CET business hours.
  - Use formal German (Sie) initially.
  - Reference DIN standards or German regulatory context.
  - Case studies from German/Austrian/Swiss companies carry weight.
  - Don't rush to close — they need to evaluate thoroughly.

### United Kingdom
- **Language**: English. Very similar to US approach.
- **Culture**: More understated than US. "Quite good" means "very good." Hierarchy matters.
- **Brexit**: Post-Brexit customs/VAT considerations for EU goods/services.
- **Legal entity**: UK Ltd or US entity with UK branch. VAT number needed.
- **Sales cycle**: 1-3 months. Faster than other EU markets.
- **Best practices**:
  - Use GMT/BST for send times.
  - British English spelling (colour, honour).
  - Reference UK-specific regulations (GDPR still applies, FCA if financial services).
  - "Limited" vs. "Inc." — use correct entity references.

### France
- **Language**: French strongly preferred. Even enterprise AEs should have French in outreach.
- **Culture**: Relationship-first, formal, intellectual. They appreciate nuance and debate.
- **Legal entity**: SAS or SARL. SIRET/VAT number needed. French invoicing standards.
- **Sales cycle**: 3-9 months. Long but relationship-driven.
- **Best practices**:
  - Personal relationship matters more than in US/UK.
  - Meetings over meals are common and productive.
  - French email sign-offs: "Cordialement" or "Bien cordialement."
  - Reference French regulations or European context.
  - Be prepared for detailed legal/contracting questions.

### Netherlands / Benelux
- **Language**: English is standard in Dutch business. Dutch appreciated but not required.
- **Culture**: Direct, efficient, flat hierarchy. High digital adoption.
- **Legal entity**: Dutch BV. EU VAT number needed.
- **Sales cycle**: 1-3 months. Fast for EU.
- **Best practices**:
  - English works well. Dutch is appreciated for warm touches.
  - Dutch companies are pragmatic — focus on ROI.
  - They value efficiency and minimal bureaucracy.
  - Reference similar Benelux companies as social proof.

### Nordics (Sweden, Norway, Denmark, Finland, Iceland)
- **Language**: English-first. Swedish/Danish/Norwegian appreciated for warm touches.
- **Culture**: Flat hierarchy, very direct, minimal small talk. Work-life balance respected.
- **Legal entity**: Most use Swedish (AB) or Danish (ApS) entities. EU VAT.
- **Sales cycle**: 1-3 months. Fast. Highly efficient buying process.
- **Best practices**:
  - Don't be too aggressive — they find it off-putting.
  - Respect business hours — no weekend/evening outreach.
  - Very data-driven buyers — bring benchmarks.
  - Sustainability/ESG messaging resonates.
  - Decisions by committee but process is efficient.

## 9. APAC Deep Dive

### Japan
- **Language**: Japanese required for any real penetration. Business Japanese is different from casual.
- **Culture**: Extremely formal. Ritual business card exchange (meishi). "Hai" ≠ agreement.
- **Legal entity**: KK (Kabushiki Kaisha) or branch office. Complex setup.
- **Sales cycle**: 6-18 months. Extremely long. Partner-led often required.
- **Best practices**:
  - Formal written communication first (email, then in-person).
  - In-person relationship building is non-negotiable.
  - Find a local partner or distributor for legal/commercial support.
  - Group consensus means everyone can veto — identify all stakeholders.
  - Gifts: Wrap carefully, avoid sets of 4 (death symbol).
  - "No" is rarely said directly — "It is difficult" means no.

### Australia
- **Language**: English. Similar to US/UK but with local charm.
- **Culture**: Laid-back, direct, wit appreciated. "No worries" culture.
- **Legal entity**: Pty Ltd (proprietary limited company). ABN required.
- **Sales cycle**: 1-3 months. Aligned with US calendars.
- **Best practices**:
  - US-friendly timezone (Sydney = +15-17 hours from US EST).
  - Reference Australian companies as social proof.
  - Aussie wit and humor in outreach works well.
  - GST applies to most goods/services.
  - Local bank accounts often needed for payment.

### India
- **Language**: English widely spoken in business. Hindi/local language for warm touches.
- **Culture**: Hierarchy-driven. Address senior person first. Price-sensitive.
- **Legal entity**: Pvt Ltd or branch office. GST registration needed.
- **Sales cycle**: 1-3 months for SMB; 3-6 months for enterprise.
- **Best practices**:
  - Pricing must be competitive — strong ROI argument needed.
  - Identify multiple stakeholders — committee buying common.
  - Digital adoption is high — email, LinkedIn, WhatsApp work well.
  - Local payment methods may be needed (UPI, bank transfer).
  - Strong tech talent pool — may compete with your product.

### Singapore / Southeast Asia
- **Language**: English primary. Mandarin for Chinese-heritage companies.
- **Culture**: Efficient, multicultural, formal. Relationship matters.
- **Legal entity**: Pte Ltd in Singapore. Regional hub model.
- **Sales cycle**: 1-3 months. Fast for Asia.
- **Best practices**:
  - Singapore is the regional hub — often where APAC decisions are made.
  - Reference regional companies (Singapore, Malaysia, Thailand, Indonesia).
  - Singapore is data-sovereign — local data residency may be needed.
  - Multi-currency: SGD, MYR, THB, IDR considerations.

## 10. International Sales Operations

### International Pricing Strategy
- **Local pricing in local currency**: EUR, GBP, BRL, MXN, AUD.
- **Price localization**: Same product, different price by market. Common in software.
- **Regional tiering**: Americas / EMEA / APAC price tiers.
- **Competitive positioning**: Check what competitors charge in each market.
- **Tax handling**: Prices typically shown as ex-VAT; local VAT added at checkout/invoice.

### International Tax Considerations
- **VAT/GST registration**: Required if you exceed thresholds or have a physical presence.
- **Withholding tax**: Some countries tax payments to foreign entities.
- **Permanent Establishment (PE) risk**: If you have employees or office in a country, you may owe taxes there.
- **Transfer pricing**: If selling between your entities (US parent → EU subsidiary), must be arm's length.
- **Stripe Tax / Avalara**: Use automated tax compliance tools for SaaS.
